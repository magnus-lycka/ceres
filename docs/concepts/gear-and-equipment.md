# Traveller Gear and Equipment

This document describes the Traveller gear and equipment concept space at the
rule and design level, based primarily on the Central Supply Catalogue and the
Field Catalogue. It is not a map of the current Ceres implementation.

## 1. Purpose and Scale

Gear is the broadest equipment category. It covers objects Travellers buy,
carry, wear, install, consume, fabricate, repair, lose, modify, and use to
change what tasks are possible. Most gear is not designed from first principles
during play; it is selected from catalogues, constrained by Tech Level, Law
Level, availability, price, mass, legality, and campaign assumptions.

A gear model should answer at least these questions:

- What is the item?
- Which source and Tech Level define it?
- How much does it cost and weigh?
- Is it legal, restricted, military, black-market, or prohibited?
- Is it available on the current world?
- Is it carried, worn, installed, consumed, deployed, or used as a tool?
- What tasks, DMs, protections, sensors, communications, software, or other
  effects does it provide?
- Does it require power, ammunition, consumables, software, training, permits,
  maintenance, or a supporting platform?

## 2. Catalogue Items, Not Full Design Systems

Central Supply Catalogue is mainly a catalogue of things to acquire. It includes
rules for availability, legality, fabrication, and some item-specific effects,
but it is not the primary design system for spacecraft, vehicles, robots, or
custom weapon construction.

That distinction matters:

- A vacc suit, medkit, portable computer, transceiver, tent, tool kit, or
  augment is a gear item.
- A ship, vehicle, or robot is usually an assembly built by its own design
  rules, even if it contains gear-like parts.
- A published weapon can be treated as gear, but Field Catalogue-style custom
  weapon design is its own specialised process.
- Some gear can also be installed into ships, vehicles, robots, structures, or
  bases, where it should keep both its equipment identity and its installation
  consequences.

## 3. Core Item Properties

Most gear needs a common set of properties:

- **Name and source**: the item identity and the book or rule source defining
  it.
- **Category**: armour, augment, computer, software, communication device,
  electronics, comfort item, medical supply, survival gear, tool, weapon,
  accessory, field equipment, or other grouping.
- **Tech Level**: minimum TL and sometimes improved versions at higher TLs.
- **Cost**: listed price, black-market multiplier, fabrication material cost,
  bulk issue cost, or source-specific adjustment.
- **Mass**: carried weight, installed weight, or negligible weight.
- **Availability and legality**: whether it can be bought openly, requires
  checks, permits, black-market access, or referee fiat.
- **Use mode**: carried, worn, implanted, installed, mounted, consumed,
  deployed, or operated.
- **Effects**: protection, skill DM, task enablement, information, healing,
  communication range, sensor range, software capability, or narrative
  permission.
- **Dependencies**: power, ammunition, batteries, consumables, raw materials,
  software, licences, tools, skill, crew, interface, or platform.

Some item tables are simple, but the concept model should be ready for items
whose rules are mostly prose.

## 4. Availability, Law, and Black Markets

Gear is not automatically present just because the characters can afford it.
Availability depends on world context, campaign assumptions, and item category.

Important constraints include:

- Local Tech Level.
- Law Level.
- Population, industry, wealth, starport class, and trade codes.
- Whether the item is specialised, military, dangerous, restricted, or exotic.
- Whether the Travellers are buying openly or through a black market.
- Whether the Referee decides the item is common, rare, or inappropriate for the
  current campaign.

Legality and availability are related but not identical. Something can be legal
but unavailable, illegal but obtainable, or technically available only through
permits, credentials, black-market contacts, patronage, or an adventure.

## 5. Categories and Rule Families

Gear categories are useful because they imply different properties and
interfaces:

- **Armour and clothing**: protection, radiation shielding, required skill,
  environmental protection, concealment, encumbrance, and compatibility.
- **Augments**: surgery, biology, installation, legality, identity, and long-term
  character consequences.
- **Computers and software**: bandwidth, rating, programs, interfaces, storage,
  hacking, diagnostics, and task support.
- **Communications**: range, encryption, transmission type, jamming, detection,
  and network compatibility.
- **Electronics and sensors**: detection modes, DMs, power, range, portability,
  and interpretation.
- **Home comforts and services**: lifestyle, lodging, survival, morale, and
  long-term travel assumptions.
- **Medical and care supplies**: diagnosis, treatment, drugs, automedics,
  consumables, recovery, and long-term harm.
- **Survival gear**: environment, atmosphere, pressure, temperature, shelter,
  food, water, and exploration.
- **Tools**: task enablement, repair, fabrication, construction, demolition,
  science, and profession-specific work.
- **Field equipment**: deployable military support, sensors, obstacles, drones,
  cooling systems, workshops, armouries, medical units, and base modules.

The categories should help the rules find the right contract without forcing
every item into the same small shape.

## 6. Carrying, Wearing, Installing, and Deploying

Gear has different relationships to the character or platform using it:

- **Carried** gear contributes to load and can be dropped, stolen, broken, or
  used directly.
- **Worn** gear interacts with armour, clothing, environment suits, biological
  bodies, and encumbrance.
- **Implanted** gear becomes part of the character and may have medical, legal,
  social, or maintenance consequences.
- **Installed** gear becomes a component in a ship, vehicle, robot, structure,
  base, or suit.
- **Deployed** gear is set up in the environment, such as mines, shelters,
  surveillance systems, field workshops, or barriers.
- **Consumable** gear is expended or depleted by use.

The same named item may need different accounting depending on whether it is in
a pack, bolted into a vehicle, installed in a robot, or deployed as a field
system.

## 7. Load, Bulk, and Practical Carrying

Mass is not the whole story. Field Catalogue emphasises that a soldier can carry
only a plausible load while still moving, fighting, and doing useful work.

Useful distinctions include:

- Everyday personal load.
- Combat-light load.
- Combat load.
- Field load.
- Overburdened load.
- Gear carried on the body versus in a pack.
- Gear that can be dropped before combat.
- Awkward bulk, even when total weight appears tolerable.

This matters most in military or exploration contexts, but the same idea applies
to Travellers carrying too many tools, weapons, spare parts, or survival systems.

## 8. Power, Consumables, and Maintenance

Many items are not self-contained in practice. They may require:

- Batteries, fuel cells, reactor power, grid power, vehicle power, or suit power.
- Ammunition, magazines, grenades, charges, tanks, filters, drugs, chemicals, or
  spare parts.
- Cooling capacity, heat sinks, radiators, rebreathers, oxygen bottles, or raw
  materials.
- Software updates, licences, data, maps, keys, permits, or calibration.
- Maintenance, cleaning, repair, sterilisation, recharging, refilling, or
  decontamination.

The catalogue entry gives the item identity, but the campaign state determines
whether the item is ready, loaded, powered, legally usable, and maintained.

## 9. Fabrication and Manufacture

Fabrication chambers provide an alternate acquisition route. They do not make
equipment free or unconstrained.

Relevant concepts include:

- Fabricator Tech Level.
- Item Tech Level and complexity.
- Chamber capacity and object size.
- Raw material requirements and cost.
- Time to fabricate.
- Design templates, patents, licences, blocks, and black-market plans.
- Restrictions on weapons, currency, controlled substances, living beings, and
  other controlled products.
- Whether the result is equivalent to a purchased item, a prototype, a copy, or
  an inferior improvised version.

Fabrication is therefore part acquisition, part manufacturing, and part legal
or logistical problem.

## 10. Field and Military Equipment

Field Catalogue expands gear into operational systems. Some field equipment is
still personal gear, but much of it is unit-level, vehicle-carried, or installed
in a base.

Examples include:

- Field armouries, workshops, barracks, medical units, and reactors.
- Surveillance systems, sensor fences, tactical communications, and drones.
- Obstacles, mines, demolition kits, and defensive systems.
- Support cooling units, ammunition handling, deployable power, and maintenance
  systems.
- Heavy equipment installed in vehicles or structures.

These items often create capabilities for a group rather than a single
Traveller. They should be treated as deployable assets with setup, crew,
transport, power, and maintenance assumptions.

## 11. Gear as Task Interface

Gear often matters because it changes task resolution:

- It permits a task that would otherwise be impossible.
- It removes an environmental obstacle.
- It grants a DM.
- It changes the timeframe.
- It changes the risk or consequence of failure.
- It supplies information, protection, healing, communication, or access.
- It allows a different skill to be used.

The item should not need to know every situation where it might be useful, but
it should expose the properties a task or domain rule needs in order to apply it
correctly.

## 12. Validation

A useful gear model should be able to check:

- The item source and version are known.
- TL, cost, mass, category, and key effects are recorded.
- Availability and legality can be evaluated when a world context exists.
- Required power, consumables, ammunition, software, or accessories are present.
- Carried and worn items fit plausible encumbrance and compatibility rules.
- Installed items respect the receiving platform's Spaces, Slots, tonnage,
  power, interface, or mounting rules.
- Deployed systems have setup, crew, area, duration, and recovery assumptions.
- Fabricated items respect fabricator capacity, TL, complexity, templates, and
  materials.

The equipment record should separate the catalogue definition from the in-play
state. A listed medkit and a depleted, damaged, stolen, illegal, or installed
medkit are not the same thing during play.
